Rhapsody in Black

Isac Friedlander | 1931

Rhapsody in Black by Isac Friedlander depicts a lively club gathering common during the Harlem Renaissance and a crucial part of Black American culture. The term Rhapsody refers to a freeflowing musical piece with rapid shifts in tempo and mood showing the change in Black culture and showing their history and what they went through.

Blues

Archibald Motley | 1929

Blues by Archibald Motley portrays a tightly packed jazz club of Harlem Renaissance. During the Harlem Renaissance, jazz became a crucial part of everyday life. The jazz club portrayed is a place where people both black and white could unite and enjoy the same entertainment because of jazz.

Let My People Go

Aaron Douglas | c. 1935 - 1939

Let my People go by Aaron Douglas represents the Old Testament of Moses leading the Israelites out of enslavement. African Americans used this as a symbolization of their plight as they migrated to the North to escape segregation prior to the Harlem Renaissance

Nightlife

Archibald Motley | 1943

Nightlife by Archibald Motley depicts a crowded cabaret in Bronzeville in Chicago capturing the vibrant nightlife and energy in Black American communities. Motley’s use of shades of magenta and violet show the diversity of the black community and culture. This scene shows the origins of Black American culture with jazz and energy.

Walking

Charles Henry Alston | 1958

Walking by Charles Henry Alston was inspired by the Montgomery bus boycotts orchestrated by the actions of unrecognized women. The abstract techniques in the painting were used to evoke the resolute energy that sustained the protest. Alston stated that “It was a very definite walk–not going back, no hesitation” he captured this by creating a striking left-to-right transition that conveys a sense of spontaneous and purposeful movement

Bondage

Aaron Douglas | 1936

Bondage by Aaron Douglas acknowledges past generations of black resilience and the hope in the face of oppression. The painting depicts the silhouettes of men and women with the most detailed object on their silhouettes being shackles. The silhouettes slouch toward ships on the shore to bring them to enslavement in an unknown country.

Charleston

Aaron Douglas | 1928

Charleston by Aaron Douglas is a commissioned illustration for the book Black Magic by Paul Morand. The book follows the story of a white woman falling in love with a black saxophonist which proved to be fatal. The noose in the middle of the view from the white women to the black saxophonist proves how their story wouldn’t work out as interracial marriages during the Harlem Renaissance were highly taboo.

From Slavery Through Reconstruction

Aaron Douglas | 1934

From Slavery to Reconstruction by Aaron Douglas depicts the history of Black Americans in America panned out on the canvas. To the left it starts with silhouettes seemingly bent by labor and exhaustion in dark towns of grey and brown. As the painting moves to the right, it gets the colors get brighter and the silhouettes stand with a sense of pride symbolizing the progression towards rights for Black Americans

Saturday Night

Archibald Motley| 1935

Bronzeville at Night by Archibald Motley depicts a jazz neighborhood common during the Harlem Renaissance. The vivid scene is enclosed by brick with no sign of nature representing the urbanization and industrialization of the 1920s. The street is illuminated by the glow of artificial light and signs that advertise against alcohol and tobacco.
